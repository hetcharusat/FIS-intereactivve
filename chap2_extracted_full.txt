Passive Attack
Ôāī A passive attack attempts to learn or make use of information 
from the system but does not affect system resources. 
Ôāī Passive attacks are in the nature of eavesdropping on, or 
monitoring of, transmissions. 
Ôāī The goal of the opponent is to obtain information that is being 
transmitted.
Ôāī Passive attacks are very difficult to detect, because they do not 
involve any alteration of the data. 
Ôāī Typically, the message traffic is sent and received in an 
apparently normal fashion, and neither the sender nor receiver is 
aware that a third party has read the messages or observed the 
traffic pattern.
Active Attack
Ôāī An active attack attempts to alter system resources or 
affect their operation.
Ôāī Whereas passive attacks are difficult to detect, 
measures are available to prevent their success. 
Ôāī On the other hand, it is quite difficult to prevent active 
attacks absolutely because of the wide variety of 
potential physical, software, and network vulnerabilities. 
Ôāī Instead, the goal is to detect active attacks and to 
recover from any disruption or delays caused by them. If 
the detection has a deterrent effect, it may also 
contribute to prevention.

Types of Cryptographic
Ôāī Cryptographic systems are characterized along three 
independent dimensions:
1. The type of operations used for transforming plaintext to 
ciphertext. 
Ôāī All encryption algorithms are based on two general 
principles: substitution, in which each element in the 
plaintext (bit, letter, group of bits or letters) is mapped into 
another element, and transposition, in which elements in 
the plaintext are rearranged. 
Ôāī The fundamental requirement is that no information be lost 
(that is, that all operations are reversible). 
Continue‚Ä¶
2. The number of keys used. If both sender and receiver use 
the same key, the system is referred to as symmetric, 
single-key, secret-key, or conventional encryption.  If the 
sender and receiver use different keys, the system is 
referred to as asymmetric, two-key,or public-key 
encryption. 
3. The way in which the plaintext is processed. 
A block cipher processes the input one block of elements at 
a time, producing an output block for each input block. A 
stream cipher processes the input elements continuously, 
producing output one element at a time, as it goes along.
Model of Symmetric Cryptosystem
Process of Cryptosystem
Ôāī In its most basic form, two people, often denoted as Alice 
and Bob, have agreed on a particular secret key. 
Ôāī At some later time, Alice may wish to send a secret message 
to Bob (or Bob might want to send a message to Alice). 
Ôāī The key is used to transform the original message (which is 
usually termed the plaintext) into a scrambled form that is 
unintelligible to anyone who does not possess the key. 
Ôāī This process is called encryption and the scrambled message 
is called the ciphertext. 
Ôāī When Bob receives the ciphertext, he can use the key to 
transform the ciphertext back into the original plaintext; this is 
the decryption process.

Congruence
‚Äļ We say that a is congruent to b modulo m, and we write a ‚Č° b 
mod m, if m divides b - a.
‚ÄĘ  Example: -2 ‚Č° 19 (mod 21), 20 ‚Č° 0 (mod 10).
‚Äļ Congruence modulo m is an equivalence relation  on the 
integers. 
‚Äď any integer is congruent to itself modulo m (reflexivity)
‚Äď a ‚Č° b mod m, implies that b ‚Č° a mod m (symmetry)
‚Äď a ‚Č° b mod m and b ‚Č° c mod m implies a ‚Č° c mod m (transitivity)

Concept of congruence

The following are equivalent
‚Äļ a ‚Č° b mod m
‚Äļ There is k őĶ Z, with a = b + km
‚Äļ When divided by m, both a and b leave the  same remainder.
‚Äļ Equivalence Class of a modulo m consists  of all integers that 
are obtained by adding a with integral multiples of m
‚Äď called residue class of a mod m

‚Äļ Residue class of 1 mod 4:
‚Äď {1, 1 ¬Ī 4, 1 ¬Ī 2 * 4, 1 ¬Ī 3 * 4,‚Ä¶}

‚Äď it has m elements, 0, 1, ‚Ä¶, m-1
‚Äď this is called a complete set of incongruent  residues (complete 
system)
‚Äď Examples for complete system for mod 5 is:
‚Äď {0, 1, ‚Ä¶, 4}, {-12, -15, 82, -1, 31} etc.
