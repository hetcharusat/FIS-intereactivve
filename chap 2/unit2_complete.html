1. Security Goals & Cryptographic Attacks

Welcome to Unit 2! Your professor took 15 hours to teach this because it lays the complete foundation for everything in Information Security. We are going to go through it step-by-step, nice and easy. We start with the why.

The Big Three: Security Goals (The CIA Triad)

Whenever we talk about securing data, we are trying to achieve three main goals. If even one of these fails, the system is insecure.

  • Confidentiality: Only the allowed people can see the message. If Alice sends an email to Bob, Eve (the attacker) shouldn't be able to read it.
  • Integrity: The message hasn't been secretly changed. If Alice says "Pay Bob $10", the attacker shouldn't be able to change it to "Pay Bob $1000" while it travels across the internet.
  • Availability: The system actually works when you need it. If a server is flooded by attackers and crashes, it is not available.
Imagine sending a physical letter through the mail:

- Confidentiality is putting the letter in a locked safe before mailing it. The mailman can't read it.
- Integrity is returning the safe with a special wax seal. If the safe was broken into and the letter changed, the broken seal is proof it was tampered with.
- Availability is the postal service actually being open and delivering the mail instead of being shut down by a protest outside their doors.

Types of Attacks (X.800 Architecture)

The ITU-T (a standard-setting organization) defined a framework called X.800 that categorizes all network attacks into two broad groups: Passive and Active.

1. Passive Attacks (Eavesdropping)

In a passive attack, the attacker just watches or listens. They do not change your data or crash your server. Because they don't change anything, these attacks are incredibly hard to detect. The focus here is on prevention (using encryption), not detection.

  • Snooping / Release of Message Content: The attacker literally just reads your emails, text messages, or bank data as it travels over the network.
  • Traffic Analysis: Even if your message is encrypted, an attacker can watch how much data you are sending, and who you are sending it to. E.g., If a military base suddenly sends a massive amount of encrypted data to front-line troops, the enemy knows an attack is coming just by looking at the traffic flow.

2. Active Attacks (Interfering)

Active attacks involve the attacker taking action. They change data, pretend to be someone else, or crash servers. Because they are doing something loud, we can detect them and recover from them, but it's very hard to prevent them completely.

  • Modification of Message: The attacker intercepts the message, changes it (e.g., changes the bank account number on a wire transfer), and forwards it to the receiver. (Attacks Integrity).
  • Masquerading (Spoofing): The attacker pretends to be someone they are not. E.g., An attacker creates a fake bank website to steal your password. (Attacks Authentication).
  • Replaying: The attacker captures a legitimate message and sends it again later. E.g., If you send a message "Unlock my car door", an attacker records that signal and plays it back an hour later to steal your car. (Attacks Integrity / Authentication).
  • Denial of Service (DoS): The attacker floods a server with millions of fake requests so that legitimate users can't access it. (Attacks Availability).
  • Repudiation: A user denies that they sent or received a message. E.g., A customer buys a stock, the stock crashes, and the customer tells the broker "I never placed that order!"
For the exam, remember this matching game:
- Passive Attacks compromise Confidentiality.
- Active attacks compromise Integrity, Authentication, and Availability.

2. Security Services & Mechanisms

To defend against the attacks we just learned about, we need Services (what we want to achieve) and Mechanisms (the actual tools/software we use to achieve it).

Security Services (What we want)

These are processing services defined by X.800 that enhance security. They directly map to our goals.

  1. Authentication: Proving you are who you say you are. "Peer Entity Authentication" proves identity at connection setup. "Data Origin Authentication" proves that an email really came from who it claims to.
  2. Access Control: After you are authenticated, are you allowed to view this file? Access control limits what users can do.
  3. Data Confidentiality: Ensuring data isn't read by the wrong people. (Protects against Snooping/Traffic Analysis).
  4. Data Integrity: Ensuring data arrives exactly as it was sent, without modification, insertion, deletion, or replay.
  5. Non-repudiation: Guaranteeing that neither the sender nor the receiver can deny the communication. (e.g., Proof of Origin, Proof of Delivery).
- Authentication: Showing your ID card to the bouncer at a club.
- Access Control: The bouncer letting you into the main dance floor, but telling you the VIP room is off-limits.
- Non-repudiation: Signing a legal contract in front of a notary. You can't claim it isn't your signature later.

3. Introduction to Cryptography & Ciphers

We've talked about the theory. Now let's talk about the math and logic behind scrambling data. We have two main characters: Plaintext (the normal, readable message) and Ciphertext (the scrambled, unreadable message).

The Basic Cryptosystem

A cryptosystem is a suite of algorithms. You need an Encryption Algorithm, a Decryption Algorithm, and a Secret Key. If Alice wants to talk to Bob, they must share the Secret Key.

Encryption: C = E_k(P) [Ciphertext equals Encryption of Plaintext using key K]
Decryption: P = D_k(C) [Plaintext equals Decryption of Ciphertext using key K]

Symmetric vs Asymmetric Cryptography

  • Symmetric Key Cryptography (Secret Key): Alice and Bob use the exact same key to lock and unlock the message. The major problem: How do they share this key safely if they are in different countries?
  • Asymmetric Key Cryptography (Public Key): Everyone has two keys. A Public Key that everyone in the world can see, and a Private Key that only you know. You encrypt with the Public Key, and only the owner can decrypt with the Private Key.
Kerckhoff's Principle is a highly tested exam concept. It states: "The security of the system must rely ONLY on the secrecy of the key, NEVER on the secrecy of the algorithm." In other words, assume the attacker knows exactly how your software works. As long as your key is a massive, secret number, you are safe.

Cryptanalysis (Breaking the Code)

How do attackers try to break our ciphers?

  1. Ciphertext-Only Attack: The hacker only has the scrambled message. They have to use frequency analysis (knowing 'E' is the most common letter in English) or brute force (trying every password).
  2. Known-Plaintext Attack: The hacker somehow got their hands on an old encrypted message and its translated plaintext. They use this pair to reverse-engineer what the secret key is.
  3. Chosen-Plaintext Attack: The hacker tricks Alice into encrypting a specific message of the hacker's choosing. By choosing the text, they can watch how the algorithm behaves and find the key.
  4. Chosen-Ciphertext Attack: The hacker tricks Bob into decrypting a specific fake scrambled message. Very powerful attack.

There are two primary ways ciphers operate to defeat these attacks: Substitution (replacing letters) and Transposition (rearranging letters). We will cover these in detail in section 5.

4. Mathematical Foundations: Modulo, Rings & Fields

Cryptography doesn't use standard math. Standard math goes on to infinity. Cryptography uses Modular Arithmetic, which circles back around like a clock.

1. Modular Arithmetic (The Clock Math)

In modular arithmetic, we are only interested in the remainder when you divide one number by another. The number we divide by is called the Modulus.

Think of a 12-hour clock. If it is 10:00 AM, and you add 5 hours, what time is it? You don't say "15 o'clock". You say "3 o'clock".
The math you just did in your head was: (10 + 5) modulo 12 = 3.
Example 1: 27 mod 5
- Divide 27 by 5. It goes in 5 times (25). The remainder is 2.
- Answer: 2.

Example 2: -18 mod 14
- In cryptography, remainders can't be negative.
- If you get -18, just keep adding 14 until you hit a positive number.
- (-18) + 14 = -4.
- (-4) + 14 = 10.
- Answer: 10.

2. Congruence

We say two numbers are "congruent modulo m" if they leave the exact same remainder when divided by m. We write this with a triple-equals sign (≡).

For example: 12 ≡ 22 (mod 10). Why? Because 12/10 leaves a remainder of 2. And 22/10 leaves a remainder of 2. They are in the same "family" in Modulo 10!

3. Groups, Rings, and Fields

These are concepts from Abstract Algebra. They sound scary, but they just tell us "what rules of math apply to our numbers?"

  • Group: A set of numbers and ONE operation (like addition). It must follow 5 rules:
    1. Closure: If you add two numbers in the group, the answer is still in the group (e.g., adding two times on a clock never gives you a time that isn't on the clock).
    2. Associativity: (A + B) + C = A + (B + C).
    3. Identity: There must be an element like '0' where A + 0 = A.
    4. Inverse: For every A, there must be a -A that gets you back to 0.
    5. Commutative: A + B = B + A (if this is true, it's called an Abelian Group).
  • Ring: Basically a group, but it supports TWO operations (Addition AND Multiplication). It follows all the rules above, plus Distributivity: A x (B + C) = (A x B) + (A x C). However, in a Ring, you might not be able to do division.
  • Field: The ultimate structure. A Field is a Ring where Multiplicative Inverses exist for every number except zero. This means you can add, subtract, multiply AND divide. Cryptography (specifically AES and public-key systems) relies heavily on mathematical Fields.
Zero Divisors: In normal math, if A x B = 0, then either A=0 or B=0.
But in modulo math, this isn't always true! For example, in modulo 10: 2 x 5 = 10 ≡ 0 (mod 10). Neither 2 nor 5 are zero, but multiplying them gets you zero! 2 and 5 are called "Zero Divisors". Fields do not have zero divisors.

5. Classical Substitution & Transposition Ciphers

In Classical Cryptography, we work entirely within Z_26 (The alphabet from A=0 to Z=25). All math is done modulo 26.

Monoalphabetic Substitution Ciphers

Each letter is substituted with exactly one other consistent letter.

1. The Additive Cipher (Shift / Caesar Cipher)

We just shift the alphabet by a key `K`.

Encryption: C = (P + K) mod 26
Decryption: P = (C - K) mod 26

If Plaintext = "HELLO" (7, 4, 11, 11, 14) and Key = 15.
'H' (7) + 15 = 22 ('W'). Very easy to crack using Brute Force (only 25 possible keys to try).

2. The Multiplicative Cipher

Instead of adding, we multiply.

Encryption: C = (P * K) mod 26
Decryption: P = (C * K_inverse) mod 26

Crucial Rule! The key `K` MUST be relatively prime to 26 (meaning GCD(K, 26) = 1). Otherwise, two different plaintext letters could result in the same ciphertext letter, ruining decryption. There are only 12 valid keys in Z_26 for a multiplicative cipher.

To decrypt, you cannot simply "divide". You must find the Multiplicative Inverse of the key (which we cover in the next section).

3. The Affine Cipher

Combines Additive and Multiplicative. Uses two keys (K1, K2).

Encryption: C = (P * K1 + K2) mod 26
Decryption: P = ( (C - K2) * K1_inverse ) mod 26

Polyalphabetic Ciphers

Monoalphabetic ciphers are easily broken using statistical frequency analysis (since 'E' always maps to the exact same letter 'X'). Polyalphabetic ciphers fix this by using multiple substitutions; 'E' might become 'X' the first time, and 'Q' the second time.

  • Vigenere Cipher: Uses a repeating keyword. If the word is "PASCAL" and message is "She is listening", we write PASCAL over and over. We add the value of the key letter to the value of the plaintext letter using modulo 26.
  • Autokey Cipher: A Vigenere cipher is weak if the enemy guesses the repeating keyword length. Autokey fixes this: The key is just one secret letter, and the rest of the key is the plaintext message itself.
  • Playfair Cipher: Encrypts letters in pairs (digraphs) using a 5x5 grid matrix created from a keyword.
  • Hill Cipher: Uses Matrix Multiplication! Blocks of letters are multiplied by a Key Matrix. To decrypt, you must find the inverse of the matrix modulo 26.

Transposition Ciphers

Instead of changing the letters (e.g. A to X), we keep the exact same letters but scramble their order. The word "DOG" remains 'D', 'O', 'G', but might become "OGD".

  • Rail Fence Cipher: Write the message diagonally up and down like a zigzag across rails, then read them horizontally row by row.
  • Columnar Transposition: Write the message in a grid from left-to-right top-to-bottom. Then, read the columns out in a scrambled order determined by a numbered key (e.g. 4-3-1-2-5).

6. Advanced Number Theory: GCD & Multiplicative Inverses

Welcome to the math that powers modern Internet Security! For ciphers and public-key cryptography to work, we need to understand how numbers divide each other perfectly.

Prime vs Relatively Prime (Coprime)

A Prime Number is a number only divisible by 1 and itself (2, 3, 5, 7, 11...).

Two numbers are Relatively Prime (Coprime) if their Greatest Common Divisor is 1. Meaning, they share no common dividing factors except 1.
Example: 8 and 15. Neither is prime (8=2x4, 15=3x5). But together, they share zero common factors. Their GCD is 1. Therefore, 8 and 15 are relatively prime to each other.

The Euclidean Algorithm (Finding the GCD)

How do we find the Greatest Common Divisor for massive numbers? We use a cascading division algorithm discovered by Euclid.

Example: Find GCD(2740, 1760)

Step 1: Divide the big by the small.
2740 / 1760 = 1 (with a remainder of 980).
We write this as: 2740 = 1 * 1760 + 980.

Step 2: Shift left! Divide the old divisor by the new remainder.
1760 = 1 * 980 + 780.

Step 3: Keep shifting left until the remainder is zero!
980 = 1 * 780 + 200.
780 = 3 * 200 + 180.
200 = 1 * 180 + 20.
180 = 9 * 20 + 0.

The very last non-zero remainder was 20. Therefore, GCD(2740, 1760) = 20.

The Extended Euclidean Algorithm (Multiplicative Inverse)

In cryptography, if you encrypt by multiplying by 7 in Modulo 26, you CANNOT decrypt by dividing by 7. Division doesn't exist natively in modulo. You must multiply by the Multiplicative Inverse.

Two numbers A and B are multiplicative inverses in Modulo N if:
A * B ≡ 1 (mod N)

Normal math inverse: If you multiply by 5 to encrypt, you multiply by 1/5 (0.2) to decrypt. 5 * 0.2 = 1. The result must equal 1.
Modulo math inverse: What number multiplied by 7 gives a remainder of 1 in Modulo 26?
7 * 15 = 105.
105 mod 26 = 1!
So the Multiplicative Inverse of 7 in Z_26 is 15!

The Extended Euclidean algorithm runs the standard Euclidean algorithm, but tracks additional numbers in a table alongside it (often recorded as t1, t2, t) to find this inverse mathematically. The crucial takeaway is: An inverse ONLY exists if the number and the Modulus are relatively prime! If GCD(A, N) is not 1, there is absolutely no inverse.

7. Euler's & Fermat's Theorems

For algorithms like RSA (which you'll learn in Unit 3), computing powers of massive numbers is required. These theorems help us simplify incredibly large exponential equations into simple numbers.

Euler's Totient Function: $\phi(n)$

The Phi-Function simply counts: "How many numbers smaller than n are relatively prime to n?"

  • Rule 1: If `p` is a prime number, every number smaller than it is relatively prime to it. So: $\phi(p) = p - 1$.
    Example: $\phi(13) = 12$.
  • Rule 2: If you multiply two prime numbers `p` and `q`, the formula is: $\phi(p \times q) = (p - 1) \times (q - 1)$.
    Example: $\phi(10) = \phi(5 \times 2) = (4) \times (1) = 4$.

Euler's Theorem

Euler discovered a magnificent shortcut. He proved that if you pick a number `a` and a modulus `n` that are relatively prime (GCD=1), then:

a^{$\phi(n)$} ≡ 1 (mod n)

How does this help? Imagine you need to solve `7^{100} mod 10`.
- We know $\phi(10) = 4$.
- Euler says: `7^4 ≡ 1 (mod 10)`.
- Since 100 is perfectly divisible by 4, we know `(7^4)^{25} = 1^{25} = 1`.
- Bam. In two seconds, without a calculator, we know the answer is 1!

Fermat's Little Theorem

This is a specific, simplified version of Euler's theorem that applies only when the modulus `p` is a prime number. If `p` is prime, and `a` is not a multiple of `p`:

a^{p-1} ≡ 1 (mod p)

Or alternatively, it can be written as: a^{p} ≡ a (mod p) for any integer a.

For the exam: Use Fermat's theorem if the modulus (the number you are dividing by) is Prime. Use Euler's theorem if the modulus is a composite number (like 10, or 30).

8. Steganography: The Art of Concealed Writing

Everything up to this point has been Cryptography: scrambling a message so it looks like gibberish. The attacker knows you are sending a secret message, but they can't read it.

Steganography is radically different. Steganography hides the fact that a message exists at all. By embedding the secret data inside of an innocent looking cover file (like a photo of your dog, or an MP3 music file), an attacker inspecting the network just thinks you are sending your friend a picture.

How Steganography works: LSB Insertion

The most common and simple technique is Least Significant Bit (LSB) Insertion.

An image is made of pixels. Each pixel has colors defined by bits (1s and 0s). The "Least Significant Bit" is the last bit of the color data. If you change a '0' to a '1' in the least significant place, the color shifts by such a tiny micro-fraction of a shade that human eyes literally cannot detect the difference. Steganography tools hide text data by weaving it into the last bits of thousands of image pixels!

Practical Self-Study Applications: Software Tools

Your syllabus mandates studying two prominent Steganography tools:

ToolCharacteristics & Usage

1. Data Hiding: Embedding text or files into innocent `.png` or `.bmp` carrier images.
2. Digital Watermarking: Embedding an invisible "fingerprint" into an image so you can prove ownership of it later if someone steals it. It is very simple, user-friendly, and great for basic data hiding.

1. Carrier Chains: Splits a huge hidden message across 10 or 20 different images at once.

3. Multi-cryptography: It encrypts the secret message with 3 different algorithms (AES, Twofish, Serpent) before hiding it in the image.
Cryptography is locking your diary in an impenetrable titanium safe. Thieves know what it is, but can't open it.
Steganography is writing your diary in invisible ink on the back of a grocery receipt. The thieves will look right at the receipt and throw it away, never knowing the diary was there.
